He Stood at the Bridge

He stood at the bridge, the man wearing his corporate suit, as he stared down at the water below. The water judged him with its lunar eyes, cold solidarity between man and nature, unparalleled in the way it induced loneliness only strengthened his resolve. He was a weird child, the black sheep of his family, outcast by no intentional means; his family was one that held their bonds above fallacious pride. Maybe he was at fault for being unknowingly vitiating, guilty by circumstances that preceded his birth? He would convince himself that their perceived indifference was something he could never control. It was a lie, designed to comfort the fall, when he would realise that it was his own indifference that made them isolate him. He had few friends, fewer meaningful connections. He was good at making them laugh, so he focused on it. Every witty prank was executed with incremental entropy as he lived for ephemeral laughs and vestiges of human born parabolas(parabola as in“smile” just in case u didn’t understand). It had been a warning left unseen, the way his friend’s eyes held amusement that was poisoned by and eventually subverted by concern. One comedian gag drawn too far, and they lurked him for it. Guilty and unable to showcase it, or understand the emotion; perceive it as more than a sting in his chest, he quietly left his circle, vanished from spots where they would share lunches and jokes. It came off as a surprise to his friends, but perceiving his departure from their side a foolish, non-apologetic action, bratty in essence, they kept their distance. He graduated at the age of 19, by hair’s breadth. He never bothered with hearing the lectures, after a professor’s chiding his appreciation for them turned into an unfair resentment. So, it did not come off as surprise when he silently made his way out of the dreducational institute. He got a job at corporate office, for marketing. He was adept at his occupation but never satisfied. Unsatisfaction turned into self hate and regret when he tried to resign, only to find that the Black company that had employed him, sought to exploit him for 10 more years; it was either that or he would have to pay back every salary, bonus he was offered. Thus, he was cornered, like a dog held onto his collor of thorns. In his withdrawal of independence, sold whatever he owned to pay the sum in installments , reducing his contract to just 7 years. He had tried to contact his family for help, but who would help someone who smiled and bowed, acknowledged only for their convenience? They never responded , and the man plunged deeper into grief. He sold his house and moved into a decrepit, old apartment in an equally dingy neighborhood. The man, his despair unending, oftentimes drunk, high on substances, kept himself at a distance from his neighbors. His neighbors too indulged in such activities, but in lieu of his cold, avoidant demeanor, only talked behind his back; for in a rotten oak barrel, every worm lurates the maggot below it. Once caught high during work hours he was quickly fired and thrown out of the building by security. Dirty, untrimmed hairs sunken, permanently red eyes. His future seemed to have extinguished under the downpour of life. He lived off of odd jobs, to feed his growing addiction. His father died. He was called to attend the funeral, the last rites demanded his presence, no matter how ungrateful he was to his family. Full of shame, he, without showing his sunken eyes, he declined his mother’s call. Sometimes, water is dangerous for far more reasons than just floods and tsunamis. Sometimes, it acts as a mirror. Not one designed to show a person slimmer or wider, but to be the harbinger of disappointments and hate that pervades minds and eats them whole. There was no one mourning for the man. It did not come off as a surprise when he became one with the reflection in the water. They sent word to his family, who moved on after not even a week, childhood clothing or toys long thrown out, there was no trace, no memory of him left.

It was as if the water had swallowed with him his existence whole, as if he had vanished into thin air.
